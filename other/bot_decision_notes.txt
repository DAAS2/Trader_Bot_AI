Step 2: Market Regime Identification (Crucial for Context)

The market is rarely static. It alternates between trending and ranging phases. Identifying the current regime is vital because indicators behave differently.

    Notes:

        Primary Tool: ADX (Average Directional Index)

            ADX Value Interpretation:

                Below 20-25: Weak or non-existent trend (ranging/consolidation).

                25-50: Strong trend.

                Above 50: Very strong trend, possibly overextended.

            DI+ and DI- (Directional Movement Index):

                DI+ > DI- and ADX rising: Uptrend.

                DI- > DI+ and ADX rising: Downtrend.

        How to use it: Your historical_adx_state and latest_adx give you this.

    Reasoning:

        Trending Markets: Trend-following indicators (MACD crossovers, moving averages) are more reliable. Oscillators (RSI, Stochastic) can stay overbought/oversold for extended periods and are less reliable for reversal signals.

        Ranging Markets: Mean-reversion strategies thrive. Oscillators (RSI, Stochastic, CCI, Bollinger Bands) are excellent for identifying overbought/oversold conditions for potential reversals. Trend indicators might give false signals.

Step 3: Indicator Interpretation & Signal Generation

This is where you translate raw indicator values into "buy," "sell," or "hold" signals. Always consider the Market Regime (from Step 2).

A. Momentum Indicators (RSI, Stochastic Oscillator, MACD Diff/Histogram, CCI)

    Purpose: Measure the speed and strength of price movements, identify overbought/oversold conditions, and potential trend changes.

    Notes & Reasoning for Signals:

        RSI (Relative Strength Index):

            Buy Signal: Price is oversold (e.g., RSI < 30).

                Reasoning: Implies price has dropped too much, likely to bounce.

                Context: Most reliable in ranging markets. In strong uptrends, RSI might dip to 40-50 and bounce (hidden bullish divergence).

            Sell Signal: Price is overbought (e.g., RSI > 70).

                Reasoning: Implies price has risen too much, likely to correct.

                Context: Most reliable in ranging markets. In strong downtrends, RSI might rally to 50-60 and fall (hidden bearish divergence).

            Divergence: Price makes a higher high, but RSI makes a lower high (bearish divergence, potential reversal). Price makes a lower low, but RSI makes a higher low (bullish divergence, potential reversal).

        Stochastic Oscillator (%K, %D):

            Buy Signal: %K crosses above %D while both are in the oversold region (e.g., below 20).

                Reasoning: Confirms upward momentum shift from an extreme low.

            Sell Signal: %K crosses below %D while both are in the overbought region (e.g., above 80).

                Reasoning: Confirms downward momentum shift from an extreme high.

            Context: Very effective in ranging markets for mean reversion.

        MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence) - macd_diff (Histogram):

            Buy Signal: MACD line crosses above Signal line (macd_diff turns positive and ideally increases), or macd_diff moves from negative to positive.

                Reasoning: Indicates bullish momentum gaining strength.

            Sell Signal: MACD line crosses below Signal line (macd_diff turns negative and ideally decreases), or macd_diff moves from positive to negative.

                Reasoning: Indicates bearish momentum gaining strength.

            Context: Excellent for trend following and identifying momentum shifts. Less reliable in choppy, ranging markets.

B. Trend Indicators (ADX, Aroon, DPO, Ichimoku, SMA, PSAR, STC, Vortex)

    Purpose: Identify the direction and strength of the price trend.

    Notes & Reasoning for Signals:

        ADX, Pos_ADX, Neg_ADX (Already covered in Step 2): Use for market regime and trend direction.

            Buy Signal (in Uptrend): ADX > 25 and Pos_ADX > Neg_ADX.

            Sell Signal (in Downtrend): ADX > 25 and Neg_ADX > Pos_ADX.

        Aroon Indicator (Aroon Up, Aroon Down):

            Buy Signal: Aroon Up crosses above Aroon Down, and Aroon Up approaches 100.

                Reasoning: Suggests a new uptrend is starting.

            Sell Signal: Aroon Down crosses above Aroon Up, and Aroon Down approaches 100.

                Reasoning: Suggests a new downtrend is starting.

            Context: Useful for identifying the beginning of a new trend.

        SMA (Simple Moving Average):

            Buy Signal: Price crosses above SMA.

            Sell Signal: Price crosses below SMA.

            Context: Basic trend filter. Can be combined with multiple SMAs (e.g., 50 SMA crossing 200 SMA for long-term trend change).

        PSAR (Parabolic SAR):

            Buy Signal: PSAR dots move below the price (indicating an uptrend).

            Sell Signal: PSAR dots move above the price (indicating a downtrend).

            Context: Excellent for setting trailing stop-losses and identifying trend reversals.

        Ichimoku (Ichimoku_A):

            Purpose: Provides comprehensive trend information, support/resistance, and momentum. ichimoku_a is the leading span A, which is part of the "cloud."

            Signals: Interpreting Ichimoku is complex, often involving Tenkan-Sen/Kijun-Sen crossovers, price relative to cloud, and cloud twists. For ichimoku_a, you'd typically compare it to ichimoku_b (which you don't have yet) to see future support/resistance.

            For your current ichimoku_a: You could use it as a dynamic support/resistance level. Price above it is bullish, below is bearish.

        DPO (Detrended Price Oscillator):

            Purpose: Removes the trend from price to identify cycles and overbought/oversold levels, often used for identifying peak/trough of cycles.

            Signals: DPO above zero suggests price is above its detrended moving average (bullish cycle), below zero suggests bearish cycle.

            Context: Best for identifying cyclical movements in non-trending markets.

        STC (Schaff Trend Cycle):

            Purpose: A leading indicator that cycles between 0 and 100, designed to identify trends and potential turns earlier than MACD.

            Signals:

                Buy: STC crosses above 25.

                Sell: STC crosses below 75.

                Strong Buy: STC at 0 and turning up.

                Strong Sell: STC at 100 and turning down.

            Context: Can be good for both trending and ranging markets due to its oscillator nature.

        Vortex Indicator (Vortex Plus, Vortex Minus):

            Purpose: Determines the presence and direction of a trend.

            Signals:

                Buy: Vortex Plus crosses above Vortex Minus.

                Sell: Vortex Minus crosses above Vortex Plus.

            Context: Good for identifying the start of a trend.

        Mass Index:

            Purpose: Identifies potential trend reversals based on the narrowing/widening of price ranges (suggesting reversal when range widens significantly).

            Signals: Usually signals a reversal when it crosses a certain threshold (e.g., 27) and then drops below a lower threshold (e.g., 26.5).

            Context: Not a trend direction indicator, but a potential reversal signal.

C. Volatility Indicators (ATR, Bollinger Bands, Keltner Channels, Ulcer Index)

    Purpose: Measure the degree of price fluctuations. Primarily for risk management (stop-loss/take-profit) and identifying high/low volatility environments.

    Notes & Reasoning for Signals:

        ATR (Average True Range):

            Use: Crucial for setting dynamic stop-loss and take-profit levels.

                Stop Loss: Entry Price - (X * ATR) for long, Entry Price + (X * ATR) for short (where X is a multiple like 1.5 or 2).

                Take Profit: Entry Price + (Y * ATR) for long, Entry Price - (Y * ATR) for short (where Y is a multiple like 2 or 3).

            Context: Higher ATR means more volatile, requiring wider stops.

        Bollinger Bands (HBand, LBand):

            Buy Signal (Ranging): Price touches or falls below the lower band (bollinger_lband).

            Sell Signal (Ranging): Price touches or rises above the upper band (bollinger_hband).

            Context: Excellent for mean reversion in ranging markets. In trending markets, price can "walk" along a band, indicating trend continuation.

        Keltner Channels (HBand, LBand):

            Similar to Bollinger Bands: Often used to identify overbought/oversold conditions relative to the average price, with channels based on ATR.

            Signals: Price touching or breaking out of channels can signal trend continuation or potential exhaustion.

            Context: Can be less prone to "false breaks" than Bollinger Bands in some cases.

        Ulcer Index:

            Purpose: Measures the depth and duration of price drawdowns from recent highs. A measure of risk.

            Use: Not typically a direct buy/sell signal. Used to assess the riskiness of an asset or strategy. Lower Ulcer Index indicates less risk/volatility in drawdowns.

D. Candlestick Patterns (Hammer, Engulfing, Doji, etc.)

    Purpose: Visual representations of price action that can signal reversals or continuations.

    Notes & Reasoning for Signals:

        Hammer (Bullish Reversal): Price makes a lower low but closes near the high, often after a downtrend.

            Buy Signal: Hammer formation after a downtrend, especially if confirmed by other indicators (e.g., RSI oversold).

        Engulfing (Bullish/Bearish Reversal): A candle body completely engulfs the previous candle's body.

            Bullish Engulfing (Buy Signal): Large bullish candle engulfs previous bearish candle, after a downtrend.

            Bearish Engulfing (Sell Signal): Large bearish candle engulfs previous bullish candle, after an uptrend.

        Doji (Indecision/Potential Reversal): Open and close prices are virtually the same.

            Interpretation: Sign of market indecision. If at a top/bottom of a trend, it can signal a potential reversal, especially if followed by a confirming candle. Not a standalone signal.

        Combining with Context: A bullish hammer is far more significant if it appears at a support level in a ranging market, or during a pullback in an uptrend, than randomly.

    How to use: Your latest_hammer_pattern_val, latest_engulfing_pattern_val, latest_doji_pattern_val give you the current candle's pattern.

Step 4: Confirmation & Consolidation of Signals

Avoid relying on a single indicator. Multiple concurring signals increase the probability of a successful trade.

    Notes:

        Example (Buy Scenario in a Ranging Market):

            IF historical_adx_state == "RANGING" AND latest_adx < 25 (Confirm Ranging)

            AND (latest_rsi < 30 OR latest_stoch_k < 20) (Oversold condition)

            AND (latest_hammer_pattern_val > 0 OR latest_engulfing_pattern_val > 0) (Bullish candlestick reversal)

            THEN Initiate BUY Trade

        Example (Sell Scenario in an Uptrend):

            IF historical_adx_state == "TRENDING" AND latest_adx > 25 AND latest_pos_adx > latest_neg_adx (Confirm Uptrend)

            AND latest_psar_up == 0 AND latest_psar_down > 0 (PSAR flips to bearish, suggesting trend exhaustion/reversal)

            AND latest_macd_diff < 0 (MACD turns negative, bearish momentum)

            THEN Initiate SELL Trade (for trend exhaustion/reversal, or short if allowed)

    Reasoning: Reduces false positives and improves trade accuracy. A single indicator can be fooled, but multiple indicators aligning provide stronger conviction.

Step 5: Risk Management (Absolutely Essential)

This protects your capital and ensures long-term profitability.

    Notes:

        Stop-Loss (SL): An automatic order to close a trade at a specific price to limit potential losses.

            Placement: Often based on volatility (ATR). For a long trade, SL might be Entry Price - (1.5 * latest_atr). For a short, Entry Price + (1.5 * latest_atr).

            Reasoning: Prevents catastrophic losses if the market moves against your prediction.

        Take-Profit (TP): An automatic order to close a trade at a specific price to secure profits.

            Placement: Also based on ATR. For a long trade, TP might be Entry Price + (2 * latest_atr) or Entry Price + (3 * latest_atr).

            Reasoning: Locks in gains and prevents emotional overholding.

        Risk-Reward Ratio: Always aim for a reward greater than your risk (e.g., a 1:2 or 1:3 ratio where TP is 2x or 3x your SL distance).

        Position Sizing: Determine how much capital to risk per trade (e.g., 1-2% of total capital per trade). This is usually calculated based on your stop-loss distance.

    Reasoning: Without proper risk management, even a highly accurate strategy can wipe out your account.
